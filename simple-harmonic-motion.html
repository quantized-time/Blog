Simple Harmonic Motion

June 2025 15 min read

A mass on a spring. A pendulum. An LC circuit. On the surface these look nothing alike — yet they are all described by the same equation. The equation of simple harmonic motion is ẍ + ω²x = 0▸, and understanding why it keeps reappearing is one of the great unifying insights of classical physics.

Where does this ODE come from? close ✕

Start with a mass m on a spring with spring constant k. Hooke's law says the restoring force is proportional to displacement: F = −kx. Newton's second law gives F = mẍ. Combining:

mẍ = −kx   ⟹   ẍ + (k/m)x = 0

We define ω² = k/m, giving ẍ + ω²x = 0. This is a linear, second-order ODE with constant coefficients. Its characteristic equation is r² + ω² = 0, with roots r = ±iω. By Euler's formula, the general solution is:

x(t) = A cos(ωt) + B sin(ωt)

where A and B are set by initial conditions (starting position and velocity).

The frequency doesn't depend on amplitude

Perhaps the strangest feature of SHM is that if you pull the spring twice as far, the oscillation takes exactly the same time. This isochrony is not obvious — a longer pull means more force, but also more distance to cover. These two effects cancel exactly▸, and the period T = 2π/ω is independent of amplitude.

Why amplitude cancels out close ✕

Write the solution as x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) for amplitude A and phase φ. Substituting into ẍ + ω²x = 0:

ẍ = −Aω² cos(ωt + φ) = −ω² · x(t)  ✓

The amplitude A appears on both sides and cancels. The equation is satisfied for any value of A. The angular frequency ω = √(k/m) depends only on the physical properties of the spring and mass — not on how hard you push.

Contrast this with nonlinear oscillators (like a pendulum at large angles), where the restoring force is no longer proportional to displacement and isochrony breaks down.

The pendulum as an approximation

A pendulum of length L obeys θ̈ + (g/L) sin θ = 0 — not quite SHM. But for small angles, sin θ ≈ θ▸, linearising the equation to θ̈ + (g/L)θ = 0. So the pendulum becomes a simple harmonic oscillator with ω = √(g/L).

The small-angle approximation close ✕

The Taylor series of sin θ around θ = 0 is:

sin θ = θ − θ³/6 + θ⁵/120 − ···

For small θ (say under 15°, which is about 0.26 rad), the cubic term contributes less than 0.26³/6 ÷ 0.26 ≈ 1% of the leading term. Dropping it: sin θ ≈ θ. The error stays below 1% for angles up to about 14°.

The approximation fails at large angles, and the period of a real pendulum grows as amplitude increases — you can measure this with a careful experiment.

Energy: an ellipse in phase space

At any instant, the total energy is the sum of kinetic and potential: E = ½mv² + ½kx²▸. This is conserved throughout the motion. In the phase plane — the space of (x, v) pairs — the trajectory is an ellipse, and the ellipse is closed precisely because energy is constant.

Proving energy conservation close ✕

Differentiate E = ½mv² + ½kx² with respect to time, using v = ẋ and dv/dt = ẍ:

dE/dt = mv·ẍ + kx·ẋ = ẋ(mẍ + kx)

The equation of motion tells us mẍ + kx = 0, so:

dE/dt = ẋ · 0 = 0

Energy is exactly constant. Substituting the solution x = A cos(ωt) and v = −Aω sin(ωt):

E = ½m(Aω)²sin²(ωt) + ½kA²cos²(ωt)
  = ½kA²(sin²(ωt) + cos²(ωt))   [since mω² = k]
  = ½kA²

So the total energy is ½kA² — proportional to the square of amplitude, and independent of time.
